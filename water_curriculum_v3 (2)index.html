Core concept

The water cycle — what you need to know

Water moves in a continuous loop. Rain falls, soaks into the ground or flows into rivers, evaporates back into the sky, and falls again.

Key idea: Water does not disappear. But it can become too far away, too dirty, or too scarce to use. Managing water means understanding where it is and how to reach it without wasting it.

Where freshwater comes from in rural communities

  • Surface water — rivers, streams, ponds. Often shared with animals. Can carry disease if untreated.
  • Groundwater — wells, boreholes. Usually cleaner, but requires effort to access.
  • Rainwater — collected from roofs or ground. Free, but only available seasonally.
Time cost of water collection: In many communities, women and girls walk 2–6 hours per day to collect water. Every hour spent collecting is an hour not spent on education, work, or rest.

WHO water standards

15 litres per person per day — minimum for basic survival.

50 litres per person per day — standard for adequate health and dignity.

How to calculate household water use

Daily total (L) = number of collection trips × litres per container. Per-person daily use = daily total ÷ number of people in household.

Agriculture insight: Livestock and crops typically use 10–20× more water than human needs. This is almost always where the largest savings are possible.

What efficiency means in water management

Achieving the same outcome with less water — or a better outcome with the same water. Efficiency eliminates waste in lower-priority uses so that higher-priority needs are always met.

Core concept

Four practical strategies

Efficiency means getting the same result with less water. These strategies work in most household settings with no additional equipment required.

Strategy 1 — Reduce collection time

  • Coordinate with neighbours — take turns so fewer people make the trip each day
  • Use larger containers to reduce the number of trips
  • Collect in the early morning when the source is least crowded

Strategy 2 — Store water safely

  • Store collected water in covered containers to prevent contamination and evaporation
  • Never store drinking water in the same container used for other purposes
  • In rainy season: collect rainwater from roofs using a clean container

Strategy 3 — Reuse water across purposes

  • Water used to wash vegetables can water plants
  • Rinse water (without soap) can clean floors
  • Animal drinking water does not need to meet human drinking standards

Strategy 4 — Reduce agricultural water use

  • Water plants in the early morning or evening — less evaporates than midday watering
  • Water the roots, not the leaves — roots absorb water, leaves do not
  • Mulching (covering soil with dry leaves or straw) keeps moisture in the ground longer
Highest-impact combination: Separating agricultural water + watering crops in the morning. These two changes alone can reduce total water use by 20–30%.

Community decisions
Key concepts — Module 4

Essential knowledge for this module

Water as a common-pool resource

A shared water source is a common-pool resource: everyone depends on it, and one person's use affects what is available for others. Common-pool resources require collective management to remain sustainable.

Four principles of effective community water management
PrincipleWhat it means in practice
Clear rulesEveryone knows what is and is not allowed near the source
Fair distributionThe burden of collection is shared — not always by the same people
Agreed monitoringCommunity members watch for violations and address them together
Conflict resolutionA process exists for resolving disputes before they damage relationships
Research across multiple countries shows that when women participate in water management decisions, outcomes improve for the entire community: sources are better maintained, rules are more consistently followed, and water is more equitably distributed.
Core concept

Water as a shared resource

Water sources are shared by everyone in a community. When one household wastes water or pollutes a source, it affects all other households.

Key idea: Individual behaviour matters, but the biggest improvements come from collective action — neighbours agreeing on rules, schedules, and responsibilities together.

Common problems and community solutions

  • Overcrowding at the source: Agree on collection times by household or area
  • Contamination: No washing, bathing, or animal watering upstream of the collection point
  • Unequal burden: Community rotas can distribute collection more fairly
  • Seasonal shortage: Agree to reduce non-essential uses in dry season
Who makes water decisions? Women and girls collect water in 80% of households worldwide, but are rarely included in management decisions. Including women consistently leads to better outcomes for the whole community.
